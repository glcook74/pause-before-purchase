The science

The science behind the pause.

Every feature is grounded in peer-reviewed neuroscience. Here's why it works.

Understanding the brain

Why ADHD brains spend differently

🧠

Dopamine deficit

Research shows ADHD involves lower dopamine receptor availability (Volkow et al., 2009). Shopping temporarily corrects that deficit — which is why the urge feels so compelling.

⏳

Delay discounting

ADHD brains systematically devalue future consequences (MacKillop et al., 2016, meta-analysis d=0.43). The future feels unreal. The checkout feels very real.

🎯

Dark pattern exploitation

E-commerce is designed to exploit exactly these vulnerabilities — urgency timers, low-stock warnings, one-click purchasing (Mildner et al., CHI 2025).

The mechanism

Why the pause works

A brief interruption between impulse and action gives the prefrontal cortex — the brain's 'brake' — time to engage. The research is clear: the brake is slow in ADHD, not absent. A 10-second pause can be enough.

Barkley (1997), dual pathway model, Sonuga-Barke (2002)

Dopamine substitution

Why we offer alternatives, not blockers

Blocking a dopamine-seeking behaviour without providing a substitute creates a motivational vacuum — and users simply override or find another way to spend. Dopamine Delay offers real alternatives: activities that activate reward pathways without financial cost.

Reward deficit model, Volkow et al. (2009); harm reduction framework

Structured delay

Why Save for Later works

Desire intensity for impulse items drops significantly within 24 hours. By creating structured delay rather than forced blocking, we work with the brain's natural de-escalation rather than against its resistance.

MacKillop et al. (2016), delay discounting literature
